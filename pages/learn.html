If you think a child may be unsafe right now, the safest step is to contact local emergency services or your local child protection hotline. You do not have to prove anything to ask for help.

Types of abuse (in safe language)

Abuse can look different in different homes. It can be a pattern, not a one time moment. If a child is being hurt, threatened, controlled, or neglected, that matters.

  • Physical harm: a child is hurt on purpose or punished in a way that causes injury.
  • Emotional harm: constant insults, threats, humiliation, or rejection that harms a child’s sense of safety.
  • Neglect: a child’s basic needs are not being met consistently (food, medical care, supervision, safe shelter).
  • Sexual abuse: any sexual behavior with a child or any sexual contact or exploitation.
  • Online exploitation: grooming, coercion, or sharing content involving minors.

Warning signs (not proof, but signals)

A single sign does not confirm abuse. But patterns can signal that something is wrong. Trust your concern, especially when multiple signs show up together or intensify over time.

  • Behavior shifts: sudden fearfulness, withdrawal, or unusually “on edge” behavior.
  • School changes: frequent absences, focus issues, or sudden drop in performance.
  • Health signals: unexplained injuries, frequent illness, or untreated medical needs.
  • Protective behaviors: avoiding certain adults, flinching, or “walking on eggshells.”
  • Adult like burden: a child taking on caregiving, secrecy, or responsibility that feels too heavy.

Your role is not to investigate. Your role is to notice, document responsibly if needed, and connect the child to safe support.

How to talk to children (calm, supportive, safe)

If a child shares something worrying, the most protective response is calm belief, no pressure, and safe next steps. Avoid interrogating. Avoid promises you cannot keep. Focus on safety and care.

What to say

  • “Thank you for telling me.”
  • “I believe you.”
  • “You did the right thing by speaking up.”
  • “This is not your fault.”
  • “We will find a safe helper together.”

What to avoid

  • “Why didn’t you stop it?”
  • “Are you sure?”
  • “Don’t tell anyone else.”
  • “I promise I won’t tell anyone.”
  • Detailed questions that feel like an interview
